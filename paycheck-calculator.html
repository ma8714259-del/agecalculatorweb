Paycheck Calculator – The Complete Guide to Understanding Your Paycheck (2026)

A paycheck calculator is a digital tool that estimates how much money actually lands in your bank account after every tax and deduction has been subtracted from your earnings. Instead of guessing what a job offer, a raise, or a new hourly rate really means for your budget, this calculator walks through the exact math that payroll departments use: it starts with your gross wages, applies federal income tax based on your filing status, layers in your state's tax rules, subtracts Social Security and Medicare, and finally accounts for retirement contributions or other withholdings you have chosen. The result is your net pay, commonly called take home pay, the number that actually matters when you are paying rent, planning savings, or comparing two job offers with different salaries and benefits.

For hourly workers, this becomes especially valuable because earnings fluctuate with hours worked, shift differentials, and overtime. For salaried employees, the calculator translates an abstract annual number into the concrete amount that will hit your account every payday, whether that is weekly, biweekly, semi-monthly, or monthly.

How Paychecks Work

Every paycheck follows the same basic structure. It begins with gross pay, the total amount earned before anything is withheld. From there, your employer applies mandatory deductions — federal income tax, state income tax where applicable, Social Security, and Medicare — along with any voluntary deductions such as health insurance premiums or retirement contributions. What remains is your net pay, the actual amount deposited into your account. Employers calculate withholding using your Form W-4, which controls how much is withheld from each paycheck throughout the year, though it does not determine your final annual tax liability.

Federal Taxes

Federal income tax is the largest deduction for most workers and operates on a progressive bracket system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates rather than your entire salary being taxed at one flat percentage. For 2026, the federal government uses seven tax brackets ranging from 10% to 37%. Before brackets apply, the IRS allows a standard deduction — $16,100 for single filers, $32,200 for married couples filing jointly, and $24,150 for heads of household — which reduces the amount actually subject to tax.

State Taxes

On top of federal tax, most states levy their own income tax, and the rules vary dramatically. Nine states — Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington and Wyoming — do not tax wage income at all. Others use a flat rate applied equally to all income levels, while many use a progressive bracket system similar to the federal government. This calculator applies an estimated state tax rate for each state to give a realistic approximation.

Social Security

Social Security tax funds retirement, disability and survivor benefits and is withheld at a flat 6.2% of gross wages, up to the 2026 wage base of $184,500. Your employer matches this contribution. Earnings above the wage base are not subject to additional Social Security tax for the remainder of the year.

Medicare

Medicare tax funds hospital insurance and is withheld at 1.45% of all wages, with no upper limit. High earners face an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on wages above $200,000 for single filers or $250,000 for married couples filing jointly.

401(k) Contributions

A traditional 401(k) contribution is deducted from your paycheck before federal and state income tax is calculated, lowering your taxable income for the year. Many employers also offer matching contributions, which is essentially free money added to your retirement account.

What is Take Home Pay?

Take home pay, or net pay, is the final amount you actually receive after every tax and deduction has been applied. It is the most practical number for budgeting since it reflects real spending power rather than a theoretical salary figure. Two people with identical salaries can have meaningfully different take home pay depending on their state of residence, filing status, retirement contributions and benefit elections.

Hourly vs Salary

Hourly employees are paid based on the exact number of hours worked and are generally entitled to overtime pay, typically 1.5 times their regular rate for hours beyond 40 in a workweek. Salaried employees receive a fixed annual amount divided evenly across each pay period regardless of exact hours worked, and many salaried positions are exempt from overtime requirements.

Common Payroll Deductions

Beyond taxes, most paychecks include health insurance premiums, dental and vision coverage, HSA or FSA contributions, life insurance, union dues and wage garnishments. Understanding which deductions are pre-tax versus post-tax explains why your taxable wages on your W-2 are often lower than your stated salary.

How to Increase Your Take Home Pay

Reviewing your W-4, maximizing pre-tax contributions to a 401(k) or HSA, claiming eligible tax credits, negotiating a higher rate, or seeking overtime opportunities are all practical ways to increase what you keep from every paycheck.

Conclusion

Your paycheck is the result of a detailed calculation involving federal tax brackets, state tax rules, Social Security, Medicare and any deductions you have elected. This calculator brings all of these moving pieces together in one place, giving you an instant, private and realistic estimate of your true take home pay for 2026, whether you are paid hourly or on salary, anywhere in the United States.

What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is total earnings before deductions. Net pay is what remains after all taxes and deductions.

The percentage of total gross income that goes toward all taxes combined, usually lower than your top marginal bracket.

Pre-tax deductions reduce taxable income before taxes are calculated. Post-tax deductions are subtracted after taxes.

Why is my net pay lower than expected?
